ASH — Vision
============

One-paragraph pitch
-------------------

Ash of the Shattered Covenant is a single-player, hand-authored ASCII RPG
set 200 years after a god-war that broke the sky into permanent floating
shards. The player wakes in a crypt with no memory, holding a blade
inscribed in a language that moves. Across five regions — the Scoured
Coast, the Glasswood, the Riven Spire, the Ash Plains, and the Hollow
Cities — they unravel who broke the sky and why their blade carries the
language of the gods. The game is presented in a truecolor terminal
using REXPaint as its authoring format, with Morrowind-level density of
named NPCs, Fallout 1/2-style dialogue, Baldur's Gate 3-style real-time-
with-pause combat, and Disco Elysium-style skill voices.

Why REXPaint + C++
------------------

REXPaint provides a CP437 + 24-bit color authoring format that maps
directly to a truecolor terminal. Hand-authored .xp files are both the
in-game world data and the artist's canvas — no separate asset pipeline,
no sprite-atlas packing, no compression pipeline. The in-game editor
is the same tool that ships with the game; there is no export step.

C++ is chosen because:
  - The game must own every byte for deterministic save/load.
  - Hand-authored content (200+ maps, ~600 NPCs, ~400 items, ~40 books)
    demands zero-copy serialization, which C++20 gives for free via
    structured bindings and consteval where useful.
  - The rendering pipeline is custom (truecolor terminal compositor),
    so a high-level engine (Godot/Unity) would be in the way.
  - entt provides ECS with no runtime overhead and no build-system pain.

What we are explicitly NOT building
-----------------------------------

  - Procedural generation. Every map is hand-authored. The world has
    edges and seams on purpose.
  - Voice acting. NPCs speak in text.
  - Sound. The slice is silent. (Post-launch: optional ambient via SDL.)
  - Multiplayer. Single-player only, always.
  - Mod tools in v1. File formats are documented for future modding
    but no official toolchain is shipped.
  - Cutscenes. Plot advances via dialogue, journal entries, and
    environmental state changes.

Reference points and what we steal from each
--------------------------------------------

  Morrowind
    - Hand-authored density over procedural size.
    - NPCs with daily schedules and names.
    - No quest markers; journals give directions.
    - Joinable factions with reputation.
    - Slow, weighty first-person movement.

  Fallout 1/2
    - Dialogue as topic list with skill-gated options.
    - Every NPC killable, death persists.
    - Special stats (Strength, Perception, ...) that gate dialogue.
    - Reputation with towns as currency.
    - Dark humor in item descriptions and dialogue.

  Fallout 3 (and the metro levels specifically)
    - Environmental storytelling via object placement.
    - Skeletons with named journals and personal effects.
    - Factional graffiti and propaganda in urban ruins.
    - Loading-screen lore as a deliberate device.

  Baldur's Gate 3
    - Real-time-with-pause combat.
    - Action economy (move + action + bonus).
    - Verticality in maps.
    - Party members with strong voiced identities.
      (We substitute: party members with strong written identities.)

  Dark Souls
    - Item descriptions that imply history.
    - Lore in load screens.
    - Weapons and armor as named artifacts with stories.
    - Difficulty as atmosphere, not punishment.

  Disco Elysium
    - Skill voices that comment on the world.
    - Failure states that are content, not punishment.
    - Thought cabinet as progression.
    - Conversation as the core mechanic.

  Ultima VII
    - Single contiguous world with no loading screens between regions.
    - NPCs who work, eat, sleep, gossip.
    - Karma as emergent property, not a number.

  Planescape: Torment
    - Dialogue as the primary content type.
    - "What can change the nature of a man?" — identity as theme.
    - Recurring NPCs across the game who remember.

What is unique to ASH
---------------------

  - The moving-language on the player's blade changes as the plot
    progresses. Reading it reveals hints. It is not a menu.
  - The world is *smaller* than Bethesda-scale but *denser* than
    anything since Ultima VII. You can read every book, talk to
    every named NPC, and the world responds.
  - Every NPC has at least one line of dialogue that changes based
    on a flag you may never know about. There are easter eggs that
    require specific combinations of actions across multiple regions.
  - Death of named NPCs is persistent and *load-bearing*. Kill the
    blacksmith before he finishes the sword he was making for you,
    and that sword is gone forever. Some NPCs carry plot items.
  - The four factions are not good/evil. They are correct in
    different ways, and the player is asked to choose which
    correctness they can live with.

Target audience
---------------

  - Players who finish Morrowind and want another 80 hours of that.
  - Players who replay Fallout 1/2 every few years.
  - Terminal-native developers and engineers.
  - ASCII art enthusiasts and roguelike players.
  - Lore-hungry readers who want every book to be worth reading.

Non-target audience
-------------------

  - Players who want voice acting, cutscenes, or 3D graphics.
  - Players who want procedurally generated content.
  - Players who want matchmaking or social features.
  - Players on phones. (The game requires a real keyboard.)

Scope realism
-------------

  Full game as specified: 3-5 years solo, 2-3 years small team.

  The plan in this directory is the complete design document. It is
  the artifact a studio would commission before greenlighting
  production. Executing it is a separate, long-term effort.

  Recommended execution cadence:
    - 2-week phases
    - Playable build at end of each phase
    - Public demo after Phase 11 (polish) covers the Scoured Coast
    - Early access after Phase 18 (factions) covers 2 regions
    - 1.0 after Phase 23 (release) covers all 5 regions + main quest

Tone
----

  The tone is solemn but not humorless. The world is broken; the people
  in it are not. NPCs tell jokes, gossip, complain about their jobs,
  fall in love, get drunk, and grieve. The moving-language on the
  blade is the only explicitly supernatural element the player sees
  in the first hour.

  Violence is consequential. Combat is not the point of the game;
  it is the obstacle course through which the player carries the
  story.

  Beauty is in the world. The Glasswood at noon is a forest of
  frozen sky-shards humming in the wind; the Riven Spire at dusk
  is a vertical city lit from below by lava-channels. The Ash
  Plains at dawn are red-gold and silent. The Hollow Cities are
  always dark.
